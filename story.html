01

The Constraint

February 2024. A borrowed laptop, $200 cloud credits, and a flood map that took 5 days to arrive—after the water receded.

Big Tech's AI? Too slow, too expensive, too generic. Their models were built for California highways, not Bangladesh monsoons.

We started with one question: How fast can we turn Sentinel-1 SAR into actionable flood maps?

02

The Physics

Water flows downhill. Epidemics follow contact networks. Markets clear when supply meets demand.

Instead of fighting these laws, we baked them into our loss functions. Penalize the model when it violates hydrology equations. Reward it when it respects price elasticity.

Result? 91% mIoU with just 47 labeled flood samples—not 47,000.

03

The Arbitrage

Silicon Valley was busy chasing AGI. We noticed something else: the 1000× cost difference between LIDAR and Sentinel-1.

Between AWS us-east-1 and a local STAC server. Between manual form processing and LayoutLMv3.

Our edge wasn't better algorithms—it was better economics. Build where others wouldn't, solve what others couldn't.

04

The Federation

Data sovereignty isn't a buzzword—it's the reason we can operate where Google can't.

Federated learning means the data never leaves the country. The model improves across hospitals without seeing patient records. Factories share insights without exposing proprietary operations.

Privacy isn't a constraint—it's our architecture.

05

The Outcome

In 90 days: 91% mIoU flood segmentation. 10,000 government documents processed. Freight forecasts with 81% R².

In 180 days: Deployments with DGHS, BRAC, and Kuehne+Nagel. Models running on everything from Raspberry Pis to satellite ground stations.

The borrowed laptop is now a distributed system. The $200 credits became revenue. The 5-day delay became 30 minutes.
